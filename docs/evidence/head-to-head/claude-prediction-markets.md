# Finding Edges and Building Auto-Trading Bots on Kalshi and Polymarket: A Practitioner's Report

## TL;DR
- **Real, documented edges exist but are narrow, fast-closing, and mostly captured by a tiny minority.** The most durable, repeatable edges are structural — internal/cross-venue arbitrage, market-making/liquidity rewards, and the favorite-longshot bias — not directional forecasting. Academic data show ~3% of Polymarket traders (plus market makers, <3.5% of accounts) capture over 30% of all gains, while ~84% of accounts lose money.
- **The APIs are mature and well-documented.** Kalshi offers a clean single REST+WebSocket+FIX API with RSA-PSS auth (CFTC-regulated, USD, full KYC); Polymarket runs a hybrid off-chain CLOB with on-chain settlement on Polygon, EIP-712 order signing, and a migration to "CLOB V2" and pUSD collateral in April 2026. Official SDKs and multiple open-source frameworks (NautilusTrader, Polymarket/agents, poly-maker) exist for both.
- **The dominant failure modes are overfitting, thin liquidity/adverse selection, capital lockup, and resolution/regulatory risk** — not lack of API access. Backtests systematically overstate returns because prediction-market liquidity swings by orders of magnitude around news, and Polymarket's UMA oracle can resolve "obvious" markets against you. Treat any backtested directional alpha with deep skepticism; treat structural/mechanical yield as the realistic base case.

## Key Findings

1. **The favorite-longshot bias is the best-documented persistent inefficiency, and it appears on Kalshi itself.** A February 2026 George Washington University / University College Dublin working paper (Bürgi, Deng & Whelan, "Makers or Takers: The Economics of the Kalshi Prediction Market"), using transaction-level data on over 300,000 contract prices across 46,282 Kalshi contracts from 12,403 events (2021–April 2025), found that contracts costing under 10¢ lose over 60% of their money, while contracts priced above 50¢ earn a small statistically-significant positive return. The average return on Kalshi contracts was about −20%. Makers earn higher returns than Takers, but both show the bias. Crucially, the authors note the bias is diminishing over time — edges decay as the market matures.

2. **Prediction-market prices are well-calibrated in aggregate but systematically miscalibrated at the extremes and in specific categories.** Per Polymarket's Accuracy page (built on Alex McCullough's Dune dashboards), odds are accurate "over 90% of the time a full month before a market resolves, and that figure rises above 96% within four hours of resolution," with a reported Brier score of 0.0843 (note McCullough's Oct 16, 2025 dashboard update put four-hour accuracy at 95.2%). But independent analysis found markets resolve at roughly the rate they're priced, with a small overestimation bias — most markets are slightly overpriced and resolve "Yes" less often than implied. Over 73% of prediction-market outcomes resolve "No," supporting systematic "bet-against-longshots" and "endgame sweep" (buying 0.95+ near-certain contracts) strategies.

3. **A Vanderbilt study casts serious doubt on efficiency during high-attention periods.** Clinton & Huang analyzed 2,500+ political markets with $2.5B in volume across IEM, Kalshi, PredictIt, and Polymarket in the final five weeks of the 2024 election. 93% of PredictIt markets predicted outcomes better than chance, falling to 78% on Kalshi and 67% on Polymarket. Prices for identical contracts diverged across exchanges, daily price changes were weakly or negatively autocorrelated (58% of Polymarket national presidential markets showed negative serial correlation — a textbook overreaction/mean-reversion signal), and arbitrage opportunities peaked in the final two weeks before Election Day. Inefficiency increased when information was most abundant.

4. **Internal (within-Polymarket) arbitrage is real and has been measured in the tens of millions.** The peer-reviewed AFT 2025 paper "Unravelling the Probabilistic Forest" (Saguillo et al., IMDEA Networks, arXiv:2508.03474) analyzed 86 million bids across 17,218 conditions (8,659 single-condition markets + 1,578 NegRisk multi-condition markets) over April 2024–April 2025 and estimated **$39,587,585 in total arbitrage profit extracted**. The bulk — roughly $28.99M — came from within-market (multi-condition/NegRisk) rebalancing, dominated by "NO"-buying strategies ($17.3M); single-condition YES+NO≠$1 arbitrage produced ~$10.58M; cross-market combinatorial arbitrage produced only ~$95K. 41% of conditions had at least one single-condition arbitrage opportunity, but only about 1% of estimated election-market opportunities were actually exploited by users. Median profit-per-dollar was around $0.60 (a ~40% gross margin), though these are dominated by thin, hard-to-fill markets. The top arbitrageur extracted ~$2.0M across 4,049 transactions. (Note: a widely-circulated secondary framing of "$29M / 73% of profits / 8.6% of opportunities / 29× capital efficiency" conflates the within-market subtotal with the total and adds ratios not found in the paper; use the paper's own figures.)

5. **Price discovery is driven by a skilled minority, and they move first on news.** The 2026 working paper "Prediction Market Accuracy: Crowd Wisdom or Informed Minority?" (Gómez-Cram, Guo, Jensen, Kung — LBS/Yale) analyzed $13.76B in volume across 1.72M accounts, 98,906 events, and 210,322 markets (2023–2025). Only ~3% (precisely 3.14%) of accounts are "skilled" with statistically significant positive PnL; combined with market makers they are fewer than 3.5% of accounts but capture over 30% of gains. Skilled traders react faster to public news (buying in the correct direction around FOMC and earnings releases before settlement; roughly an 8-basis-point rise in correct-outcome probability per 1 percentage point of skilled net buying) and eliminate law-of-one-price violations; skill persists (44% cross-sample vs ~10% for mutual-fund managers). A separate SSRN study (Akey et al.) of $67B in volume found the top 1% of profitable users capture 76.5% of profits, and that winners provide liquidity via limit orders while losers take liquidity via market orders.

## Details

### PART 1 — Where the edges are

**(a) Statistical / structural inefficiencies.**
- *Favorite-longshot bias:* Documented on Kalshi (GWU/UCD paper above) and, more weakly, on Polymarket. Note nuance: Reichenbach & Walther (SSRN, Dec 2025), using 478M+ Polymarket trades, found *no* general longshot bias and that prices "slightly outperform bookmaker odds," but did find a tendency to overtrade the default and "Yes" option. The practical read: the bias is real and tradeable on Kalshi's tails and in less-liquid categories, but it is competed away in Polymarket's deepest markets. Betting heavy favorites and buying cheap "No" across many longshots is the canonical expression; expect thin, decaying margins.
- *Resolution-date convergence / time decay:* Prices converge to 0/1 as resolution approaches; accuracy jumps in the final hours. The "endgame sweep" (buying 0.97–0.997 contracts hours before settlement) trades time for near-certain small profit but carries black-swan tail risk — practitioners cap position size (e.g., ≤10% per market) precisely because of this.
- *Overreaction / mean reversion:* The negative serial correlation documented by Clinton & Huang (58% of Polymarket presidential markets) is a genuine short-horizon mean-reversion signal, strongest in low-liquidity and high-hype conditions. The older InTrade "Yogi Berra bias" (losing sports teams priced too high in the final minutes) is the classic analogue.
- *Stale quotes / liquidity gaps:* On thin markets one side of YES/NO lags the other after news; the sum drifts from $1. These are the raw material of both internal and cross-venue arbitrage.

**(b) Cross-venue arbitrage (Kalshi ↔ Polymarket ↔ sportsbooks/PredictIt).**
The mechanic: find an economically-equivalent binary event, buy the cheap YES on one venue and the cheap NO on the other so the combined cost is < $1 net of fees. Documented realistic net edges after fees are small (often 1–2% after a 6% gross spread compresses), and windows on liquid markets close in seconds to minutes. Community/vendor guides converge on 15–40% annual returns on deployed capital in documented sustained cases, highly variable. Key mechanics to get right:
- *Contract matching:* Fuzzy-match team names, dates, and market types; a Kalshi ticker like `26FEB13ARSCHE` must map to the same Arsenal-Chelsea match on Polymarket. Verify resolution criteria match exactly — "GDP growth > 3%" can reference different measures/revisions; a mismatch turns "arbitrage" into a directional double-loss.
- *Fees:* Kalshi taker fee = `ceil(0.07 × contracts × P × (1−P))`, peaking at 1.75¢/contract at 50¢ and tapering to near-zero at the tails; maker fees are ~25% of taker (or zero on many markets, though Kalshi began charging some maker fees after April 2025). Polymarket charges zero fees on most markets, with taker fees only on a subset (crypto up/down, some NCAAB and Serie A markets). This fee asymmetry is *why* the arbitrage exists — Polymarket is usually the "cheap" leg.
- *Settlement/timing & capital lockup:* You need USD pre-funded on Kalshi (ACH 1–3 days) and USDC/pUSD pre-funded on Polygon simultaneously. Capital efficiency is low — most funds sit locked until resolution. Best categories per practitioner reports: mid-tier political/state markets, economic indicators (CPI, jobs), sports where coverage overlaps. Worst: major elections and crypto (bot-saturated, spreads <0.5–2%).
- *Sportsbook cross-book:* Comparing prediction-market prices to sharp books (Pinnacle/PS3838) via implied probability can produce larger edges because the crowds differ; sharp books are near-perfectly calibrated, so this is closer to "the book is right, the prediction market is wrong" than true two-sided arb.

**(c) Internal / structural arbitrage within one venue.**
- *YES/NO spread arb:* Within a single binary market, if YES + NO < $1 net of fees, buy both for locked profit. Bot-dominated; windows close in seconds on liquid markets.
- *Multi-outcome coherence (NegRisk):* In a mutually-exclusive set (e.g., "election winner"), all YES prices should sum to ~$1. If the sum < $1, buy one of each YES for guaranteed profit; if the sum of a dependent subset > $1, the NOs are underpriced. This "sum-less-than-100%" strategy is how one documented address reportedly turned $10K into $100K over 10,000+ markets. Polymarket's `negRisk` boolean (Gamma API) flags these; the NegRiskAdapter contract lets a NO in any market convert to YES in all others for capital efficiency. **Critical distinction:** NegRisk provides *capital efficiency* (buy all outcomes for $1.00 collateral instead of the sum), not arbitrage — true arbitrage requires the standard interface when the sum is genuinely < $1. Getting this backwards destroys the edge.
- Note that all such trades are non-atomic on an order book, so there is always execution/leg risk.

**(d) Calibration analysis.** Build calibration curves and Brier scores from historical resolution data to find chronically mispriced categories. Documented findings to exploit or avoid: sports on Polymarket trails sharp books (treat as cross-reference, not truth); politics/geopolitics is where the crowd has a genuine edge over any single institution; niche/"will X say word Y" markets are least accurate and most speculative; low-liquidity markets are systematically overpriced.

**(e) Information edges.** Faster news/data feeds (polling, live sports APIs, economic releases, weather) let you trade before prices update. One report cites automated systems executing 10,200+ bets for $4.2M in 2024–25 purely on speed. This is an information-latency edge, not risk-free arbitrage. Weather markets on Kalshi are a documented niche for data-driven bots (e.g., the "Predict & Profit" weather bot). Around scheduled releases (FOMC, CPI), the skilled-minority paper shows informed flow moves first — you are competing against them.

**(f) Market-making vs directional.** Market-making captures the spread and earns Polymarket **liquidity rewards** — a standalone daily PUSD payout (paid ~midnight UTC, $1/day minimum) scored by a *quadratic* function of proximity to the midpoint, two-sided depth, order size, and uptime. Rewards accrue whether or not orders fill, but the highest-scoring orders (tightest to mid) are also the most likely to be adversely selected. Realistic returns: one detailed postmortem targeted ~10% annualized on calm, long-dated markets and warned that rewards are now "a thin bonus on top of real trading edge," not a money printer; peak early-era reports of $200–300/day on $10K capital have compressed. Kalshi pays 3.75% APY on both cash and open positions for accounts with a $250+ balance (variable, accrues daily, paid monthly) — the program launched near 4.05% in October 2025 and 4.00% (expanded to positions) in August 2025 before settling at 3.75% by mid-2026 — partially offsetting fees for low-turnover traders. Inventory management (skew, widening spreads into thin hours 3–7 AM UTC, kill switches during news) is the core skill — the difference between "the dumb money" (>40% adverse-fill ratio) and a profitable maker (<25%).

**(g) Academic literature summary.** The efficiency picture is genuinely mixed: Iowa Electronic Markets showed no favorite-longshot bias; InTrade showed longshot overpricing >10 days out (a discounting effect, per Page & Clemen); Kalshi shows a clear favorite-longshot bias that is diminishing over time; Polymarket is well-calibrated in aggregate but inefficient during high-attention periods and driven by a ~3% skilled minority. The consensus: prices are informative and improve toward resolution, but persistent inefficiencies live in the tails, in thin/niche markets, in structural coherence violations, and in short-horizon overreaction.

### PART 2 — Building the bots

**Kalshi API.**
- Base URL `https://api.elections.kalshi.com/trade-api/v2`; WebSocket `wss://api.elections.kalshi.com/trade-api/ws/v2`; full demo/sandbox environment at `demo-api.kalshi.co`. Also offers FIX 4.4 (FIXT.1.1) for institutional low-latency.
- **Auth:** API key ID + RSA-PSS request signing (SHA-256, MGF1, salt length = digest length = 32 bytes). Sign the string `timestamp_ms + METHOD + path` (path includes `/trade-api/v2`, excludes query string); send `KALSHI-ACCESS-KEY`, `KALSHI-ACCESS-TIMESTAMP` (ms), `KALSHI-ACCESS-SIGNATURE` (base64) headers. Common traps: signing the path with query params, using seconds not ms, clock drift. No wallet, no session token, no refresh flow.
- **Order types:** limit and market orders, plus batch create/cancel, amend, decrease. Prices are integer cents 1–99 (migrating to fixed-point `_fp` and `_dollars` fields; legacy integer fields removed March 12, 2026; fractional trading rolling out per-market from March 2026). Max 200,000 open orders per user.
- **Rate limits:** token-bucket model, tiered. Basic tier ~20 read / 10 write per second (community-reported); buckets hold ~2 seconds of budget and allow bursting; write bucket capacity is 2× the per-second budget above Basic. Batch orders cost 10 tokens per order. Self-upgrade to Advanced via `POST /account/api_usage_level/upgrade`; volume-based tiers exist. Separate independent buckets for event-contract vs perps, read vs write. WebSocket is read-only for market data (orders go via REST/FIX).
- **Historical data:** Kalshi now partitions live vs historical; use `GET /historical/markets`, `/historical/trades`, `/historical/markets/{ticker}/candlesticks`, and `GET /historical/cutoff` for the boundary. Kalshi's live API does **not** retain historical order-book state.
- **Compliance:** CFTC-regulated Designated Contract Market; requires full KYC and a funded USD account (ACH/wire free, debit 2% fee). Programmatic/automated trading is explicitly supported. Only fully-legal venue for US persons among the two.

**Polymarket API.**
- Three APIs: **CLOB** (trading/order book, `https://clob.polymarket.com`, chain_id 137), **Gamma** (market discovery/metadata, token IDs, `negRisk` flags), and **Data** (positions/history). Plus a WebSocket for market/user/sports channels and RTDS for low-latency.
- **CLOB V2 hard cutover April 28, 2026:** new Exchange contracts, EIP-712 signing domain bumped v1→v2, new order struct, new fee model, **pUSD collateral** (ERC-20 backed 1:1 by USDC, replacing USDC.e), and new V2 SDK packages (`py-clob-client-v2`, `@polymarket/clob-client-v2`). V1 SDKs and V1-signed orders no longer work. Old Goldsky subgraph indexing was also retired.
- **Auth (two layers):** L1 = EIP-712 wallet signature to create/derive API credentials (proves wallet ownership); L2 = API key + HMAC-SHA256 request signing for fast trading (`POLY_ADDRESS`, `POLY_API_KEY`, `POLY_PASSPHRASE`, `POLY_SIGNATURE`, `POLY_TIMESTAMP`). Orders themselves must additionally be EIP-712 signed by the wallet.
- **Signature types & wallet setup — the single biggest integration trap:** signature_type 0 = plain EOA, 1 = POLY_PROXY (email/Magic proxy), 2 = Gnosis Safe browser wallet, 3 = POLY_1271 deposit wallet (recommended for new API users). The `funder` address (holds funds) differs from the signing EOA when using proxies. Documented open bugs: POLY_PROXY frontend-generated keys fail V2 auth (signer/proxy address mismatch, 401s), and POLY_1271 L1 auth binds the key to the EOA not the deposit wallet. New users' most reliable path has been email→Magic wallet→extract key→signature_type=1, which scales poorly. EOA/MetaMask users must set token allowances (approve pUSD + CTF contract) before trading.
- **Order types:** limit, market (FOK/FAK), batch (up to 15 per call), post-only. Binary options support up to 6-decimal amounts; market orders have stricter precision.
- **Settlement:** off-chain matching, on-chain settlement on Polygon via the CTF (ERC-1155 conditional tokens). Gas paid in POL; deposit wallets can be deployed gaslessly via a relayer. Polygon checkpoints to Ethereum ~every 30 min for finality.
- **Resolution:** UMA Optimistic Oracle. A proposer posts a ~$750 USDC.e bond; 2-hour challenge window; disputes escalate to a second proposal and then UMA's DVM token-holder vote (4–6 days). ~99% of assertions go undisputed. **This is a material risk** — see below.
- **Rate limits:** Cloudflare-fronted; switch to WebSockets (don't count against REST limits); batch orders; cache Gamma metadata; watch `X-RateLimit-Remaining`.

**Open-source frameworks & repos.**
- *Kalshi:* `Kalshi/kalshi-starter-code-python` (official examples, not an SDK); official auto-generated `kalshi_python_sync` / `kalshi_python_async` / `kalshi-typescript` SDKs (old `kalshi-python` deprecated); community `arshka/pykalshi` (WebSocket streaming, retries, pandas), `TexasCoding/kalshi-python-sdk` (full 101-operation coverage + perps + FIX), `humz2k/kalshi-python-unofficial`.
- *Polymarket:* official `Polymarket/py-clob-client` / `py-clob-client-v2` and `@polymarket/clob-client-v2`; `Polymarket/agents` (MIT-licensed AI-agent framework with Gamma connector, Chroma vector DB, LLM/news integration); `Polymarket/polymarket-subgraph`.
- *Cross-venue / production:* **NautilusTrader** (`nautechsystems/nautilus_trader`) — production-grade Rust-native, event-driven engine with an official Polymarket adapter (`pip install` with `[polymarket]` extra), research-to-live parity, backtesting on the same code; the strongest foundation for a serious bot. Community market-makers: `warproxxx/poly-maker` (maker-only CLOB V2 MM for political markets, fair-value + inventory-skew, regime machine, kill switches, paper mode), `warproxxx/poly_data` (on-chain data retriever via HyperSync post-subgraph-retirement), `chainstacklabs/polymarket-alpha-bot` (covering-portfolio arb across correlated markets with LLM). Treat vendor "arb finder" and RL bots (e.g., Polymarket-RL-Agent) as learning resources — one reviewer's $500 live RL test over two weeks ended at $487 (flat after gas).

**Architecture patterns.** Standard production stack: (1) **data ingestion** — WebSocket streaming with a locally-maintained order book from deltas, REST only for snapshots/backfill; (2) **signal generation** — separate from execution; (3) **OMS** — idempotent, never blind-retry POST/DELETE (duplicate-order risk); (4) **risk/position management** — per-market position caps, daily-loss kill switch, inventory skew, dead-man's-switch heartbeat; (5) **backtesting** — event-driven, same code as live; (6) **monitoring** — Grafana/Slack alerts, adverse-fill tracking. Run on a low-latency VPS near the venue.

**Backtesting & historical data.**
- *Kalshi:* official historical endpoints (candlesticks/trades, no book state); third-party `kalshibacktest.com` archives order-book snapshots at sub-second resolution (free tier newest-50 markets, Pro $19.90/mo for 31 days history).
- *Polymarket:* on-chain via HyperSync (Envio, free API token) or Goldsky Turbo Pipelines (post-V2, subgraphs deprecated April 2026); The Graph subgraph (100K queries/mo free); third-party `polymarketdata.co` (S3 exports, minute-level price history + L2 book snapshots from Aug 2025) and `pmdata.dev` (tick-level parquet for crypto up/down markets). The official public API returns *live state only* — no historical endpoint.

**Validating a strategy (avoiding self-deception).** The reproducibility crisis is acute — over 90% of academic strategies fail live. Mandatory practices:
- **Walk-forward analysis only** (never k-fold on time series — it leaks the future). Freeze parameters, roll forward, compute Walk-Forward Efficiency (out-of-sample ÷ in-sample return).
- **Correct for multiple testing:** Deflated Sharpe Ratio and Probability of Backtest Overfitting (Bailey et al.); trying 100 variants and picking the best ≠ an edge.
- **Realistic fills:** backtests use mid-quote; live crosses the spread. Prediction-market liquidity is event-dependent — the "Anatomy of Polymarket" paper documented Kyle's lambda dropping ~50× (a $1M order moving ~13pp early in the 2024 cycle vs ~0.25pp by October). A backtest ignoring this mis-models fills by orders of magnitude. Model slippage 0.1–1%+ (higher in stress), partial fills, and the accelerating illiquidity in a contract's final 30 minutes.
- **Account for all costs:** Kalshi fees, Polymarket gas, capital lockup (a 2% arb over 90 days is a poor annualized return), and bid-ask.
- **Paper-trade before going live** (both NautilusTrader and community bots default to paper mode), then go live with small size.

**Practical constraints & risk.**
- *Capital:* practitioners suggest $2,000–5,000 minimum split across venues to meaningfully capture cross-venue spreads; more for market-making.
- *Liquidity:* many markets are thin — a single $1,000 order can move a thin leg from the low-40s to high-60s before filling, evaporating the spread. This is the #1 practical killer of paper-profitable arb.
- *Adverse selection:* your maker orders fill precisely when the fair value has moved against you (Sharp Steve vs Noisy Nick). Documented Kalshi microstructure analysis frames this as the central maker risk.
- *Resolution risk:* Kalshi resolves per published rulebook (CFTC-supervised); Polymarket's UMA oracle can resolve against the "obvious" outcome. Per the Wall Street Journal's May 18, 2026 investigation "The Mysterious Crypto Judges Who Settle Polymarket Disputes": "In most disputes, more than 50% of the votes are concentrated in the 10 largest wallets"; "at least 60% of active UMA voters could be directly linked to Polymarket accounts"; and "In more than 300 disputes—or nearly one in five during that period—at least one UMA voter had a financial stake in the outcome." UMA resolves on the "spirit"/headline of the market, not lawyerly rule-reading, and Polymarket clarifications are binding. High-profile disputes illustrate real payout uncertainty — e.g., the "MicroStrategy sells any Bitcoin by May 31, 2026?" contract (>$60M volume), disputed twice after a June 1 8-K disclosed 32 BTC sold May 26–31 at an avg net price of $77,135, the largest UMA dispute since the ~$237M Zelenskyy-suit market (The Defiant, June 2026). Cross-venue arb assumes both venues resolve identically — they can and do differ, turning a "locked" arb into a double loss.
- *Regulatory/geographic:* **Kalshi** — CFTC-regulated DCM, legal in all 50 states for US persons. **Polymarket** — two distinct products: *Polymarket Global* (offshore, USDC/Polygon, no KYC) has been **geoblocked for US IPs since the January 2022 CFTC settlement**; per CFTC Press Release 8478-22 (Jan. 3, 2022) the order against Blockratize, Inc. d/b/a Polymarket required it to "pay a $1.4 million civil monetary penalty," wind down non-compliant markets, and cease-and-desist, and Polymarket began geoblocking US users on Jan. 12, 2022. VPN circumvention violates its ToS and risks account/fund freezes and permanent bans (the Van Dyke prosecution is the cautionary case). *Polymarket US* (QCX LLC), a CFTC-registered DCM acquired for $112M, launched **December 3, 2025** after a November 25, 2025 Amended Order of Designation — full KYC, USD, intermediated via FCMs, US-only. US persons must use Kalshi or Polymarket US. Note also state-level action — Minnesota's SF4760 (omnibus public-safety law), signed by Gov. Tim Walz on May 18, 2026, makes it a felony to operate, support, or advertise a prediction market, effective August 1, 2026; per NPR (May 19, 2026) it is "the nation's first law banning prediction market sites," and both the CFTC (CFTC v. Minnesota) and Kalshi sued seeking a preliminary injunction — plus 20+ countries restricting Polymarket Global.
- *Tax:* event-contract tax treatment is nuanced; trading fees are generally deductible investment expenses. Consult a professional.

## Recommendations

**Stage 0 — Decide your venue and legal posture first.** If you are a US person, you are effectively limited to Kalshi (simplest: USD, RSA auth, no wallet) and/or Polymarket US. Do not attempt VPN access to Polymarket Global — the regulatory and account-seizure risk dominates any edge. This single fact should shape everything downstream.

**Stage 1 — Build read-only infrastructure and a calibration study (weeks 1–4).** Stand up authenticated read access on both venues, stream order books via WebSocket into a local store, and reproduce the published calibration/favorite-longshot findings on your own historical data (Kalshi historical endpoints + kalshibacktest.com; Polymarket via HyperSync/Goldsky). *Benchmark that changes the plan:* if you cannot reproduce the favorite-longshot bias or a stable calibration miscalibration in a category on your own data, do not trade it directionally.

**Stage 2 — Start with mechanical, market-neutral strategies, not directional alpha.** In priority order: (1) internal NegRisk/YES-NO coherence arbitrage on Polymarket (measured at ~$40M/yr extracted, but you're competing with bots — target thin, newly-created, or just-changed multi-outcome sets); (2) cross-venue arb on mid-tier political/economic/sports markets (avoid bot-saturated major elections and crypto); (3) liquidity-reward farming / market-making on Polymarket calm long-dated markets as a yield overlay. Realistic expectation: single-digit-to-low-double-digit annualized on deployed capital, with meaningful capital lockup — *not* the headline arb percentages.

**Stage 3 — Only add directional/information edges after mechanical strategies are profitable live.** Weather (Kalshi) and fast news/data-feed strategies are the most defensible directional niches. Validate with strict walk-forward + Deflated Sharpe + realistic fills including the 50× liquidity swing. *Benchmark:* require out-of-sample Walk-Forward Efficiency > ~0.5 and profitability net of modeled slippage/fees before risking size.

**Stage 4 — Go live small, instrument everything.** Use NautilusTrader (paper → live parity) or a well-reviewed community bot as the base. Hard risk caps, daily-loss kill switch, adverse-fill monitoring (<25% target), and per-market position limits (≤10% of capital) from day one. Scale only as live results confirm backtested assumptions.

**Thresholds that should make you stop or pivot:** adverse-fill ratio persistently >40% (you are the informed traders' counterparty); live returns <½ of backtest (overfit or fill-optimistic); any single UMA resolution loss on a "sure thing" (re-price your resolution-risk model); cross-venue legs repeatedly failing to fill simultaneously (execution risk exceeds edge).

## Caveats
- **Speculative vs proven:** The favorite-longshot bias (Kalshi), internal arbitrage magnitude (~$40M/yr on Polymarket), skilled-minority price discovery, and profit concentration (top 1% capture 76.5%) are peer-reviewed or transaction-level-documented. Vendor claims of "15–40% annual arb returns," specific bot P&Ls, and "$4.2M from 10,200 bets" come from commercial/blog sources and should be treated as illustrative, not verified. The Gómez-Cram et al. paper is a 2026 working paper (not yet peer-reviewed).
- **Edge decay is explicit:** the GWU/UCD authors state the Kalshi bias is diminishing over time, and Polymarket liquidity rewards have compressed from their early-era peaks. Any documented edge is being competed away.
- **The APIs are moving targets:** Polymarket's V2 cutover (April 2026), pUSD migration, and Kalshi's fixed-point/fractional-trading migration (March 2026) mean code and field names change frequently. Treat the OpenAPI/AsyncAPI specs as source of truth over any SDK or third-party guide, and expect the signature-type/wallet setup on Polymarket to be the hardest part to get working.
- **Most participants lose.** ~84% of Polymarket accounts are unprofitable; only ~0.03% ever cleared $100K. The realistic outcome for a solo builder is small structural yield with high operational overhead, not durable directional alpha. Size and expectations accordingly.
- Conflicting evidence exists on the Polymarket longshot bias (present in some studies, absent in Reichenbach & Walther's 478M-trade sample) — resolve it empirically on your own data before trading it.
