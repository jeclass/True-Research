# Daily Low-Dose Aspirin for Primary CV Prevention in Adults Over 60

## Bottom line

The evidence does **not** support daily low-dose aspirin for primary prevention of a first cardiovascular event in adults over 60 who do not have existing heart disease. The major randomized trials show no significant reduction in first cardiovascular events in this age group while showing a clear, statistically significant increase in major bleeding. Accordingly, current major guidelines — USPSTF (2022), ACC/AHA (2019), and ESC (2021) — advise against routine initiation of aspirin for primary prevention in older adults, and the USPSTF advises considering stopping it around age 75 in those already taking it [src-effect-of-aspirin-on-cardiovascular-even][src-aspirin-and-statin-therapy-for-primary-p][src-new-uspstf-recommendation-on-aspirin-in][src-editorial-the-2021-european-society-of-c][src-potential-impact-of-the-2019-acc-aha-gui].

## Trial evidence: effect on first cardiovascular events

**ASPREE** enrolled 19,114 healthy adults aged ≥70 (≥65 for Black/Hispanic participants), median age 74. Daily low-dose aspirin did not significantly reduce first cardiovascular events: the hazard ratio for the composite of fatal CHD, non-fatal MI, stroke, or heart-failure hospitalization was 0.95 (95% CI 0.83–1.08), about a 5% relative reduction that was not statistically significant [src-effect-of-aspirin-on-cardiovascular-even]. The absolute risk difference for composite CVD was 0.6 events per 1,000 person-years — roughly a 0.06% annual absolute risk reduction [src-assessing-aspirin-for-primary-cvd-preven]. For major adverse cardiovascular events (fatal CHD, non-fatal MI, ischemic stroke) the HR was 0.89 (95% CI 0.77–1.03), and individual MI and ischemic stroke components had HRs of 0.93 (95% CI 0.76–1.15) and 0.89 (95% CI 0.71–1.11) respectively — none statistically significant [src-effect-of-aspirin-on-cardiovascular-even]. Aspirin did not reduce the absolute incidence of first cardiovascular events in ASPREE [src-assessing-aspirin-for-primary-cvd-preven].

**ARRIVE** enrolled 12,546 moderate-risk adults (men ≥55, women ≥60). Daily aspirin produced a HR of 0.96 (95% CI 0.81–1.13) for first major cardiovascular events (CV death, MI, unstable angina, stroke, TIA), an absolute difference of –0.19 percentage points over five years — essentially no benefit. No age subgroup analysis was reported [src-use-of-aspirin-to-reduce-risk-of-initial].

**ASCEND** enrolled 15,480 adults with type 1 or 2 diabetes (mean age 63) and no prior CVD, followed for a median 7.4 years. Daily aspirin reduced the composite first vascular event (MI, stroke/TIA, or vascular death) by about 12% relative risk (HR ≈0.90; 95% CI 0.79–0.97), an absolute reduction of 1.1 percentage points [src-a-study-of-cardiovascular-events-in-diab][src-studies-that-changed-clinical-practice-a].

Taken together, all three landmark RCTs found no net benefit of low-dose aspirin for primary prevention in adults over 60, alongside an increased risk of major bleeding [src-aspirin-and-statin-therapy-for-primary-p].

## Bleeding and other harms

Daily low-dose aspirin significantly increases bleeding risk. In observational studies of adults over 60, the pooled relative risk was 1.4 for any GI bleeding (95% CI 1.2–1.7), 2.3 for upper GI bleeding, and 1.4 for intracranial hemorrhage [src-bleeding-risk-with-long-term-low-dose-as]. In ASPREE, major bleeding was significantly higher in the aspirin arm (HR 1.38; 95% CI 1.18–1.62), and a secondary analysis found an HR of 1.38 (95% CI 1.03–1.84) for any intracranial hemorrhage [src-effect-of-aspirin-on-cardiovascular-even-2][src-low-dose-aspirin-and-the-risk-of-stroke].

On how risk changes with age: the ASPREE intracranial-bleeding analysis found no significant age-by-treatment interaction across the 65–74, 75–84, and ≥85 subgroups, indicating the *relative* increase in bleeding does not vary appreciably with age beyond 60 [src-low-dose-aspirin-and-the-risk-of-stroke]. However, observational Cosmos data show aspirin associated with a 33–37% higher major-bleeding risk among those aged ≥75, with no increased risk seen in younger adults [src-low-dose-aspirin-usage-for-primary-preve].

On mortality: ASPREE did not show an overall mortality benefit from aspirin; the original trial reported a slightly higher all-cause mortality among aspirin users, a signal not confirmed in subsequent analyses [src-daily-aspirin-recommendation-should-we-a][src-low-dose-aspirin-increases-risk-of-bleed].

## What the guidelines recommend

**USPSTF (2022)** — current as of June 2026 — advises against initiating low-dose aspirin for primary prevention in adults aged ≥60 (Grade D, harms outweigh benefits) [src-new-uspstf-recommendation-on-aspirin-in][src-aspirin-use-to-prevent-cardiovascular-di-2][src-should-my-older-adult-patients-take-aspi]. For adults 40–59 with a ≥10% 10-year ASCVD risk, it gives an individualized Grade C recommendation favoring shared decision-making [src-new-uspstf-recommendation-on-aspirin-in][src-aspirin-for-primary-prevention-of-cardio]. The rationale draws on a meta-analysis of 13 RCTs (>160,000 participants): in those aged ≥60 the absolute CV risk reduction was only about 0.38–0.41% (NNT≈250) while major bleeding rose by about 0.47%, yielding net harm [src-aspirin-for-primary-prevention-of-cardio][src-aspirin-for-the-primary-prevention-of-ca]. For those already taking aspirin, the USPSTF suggests net benefit declines with age and proposes stopping around age 75, or earlier if bleeding risk is high [src-uspstf-recommends-against-initiating-asp][src-daily-low-dose-aspirin-for-heart-attack][src-uspstf-updates-recommendations-on-aspiri].

**ACC/AHA (2019)** classifies low-dose aspirin for primary prevention as Class III (harm) in adults aged ≥70 (also stated as >70 in some sources) and as Class IIb (may be considered) for selected adults aged 40–70 at higher CVD risk without increased bleeding risk [src-uspstf-report-on-aspirin-for-primary-pre][src-aspirin-yes-for-at-risk-elderly-but-what][src-potential-impact-of-the-2019-acc-aha-gui]. The guideline states aspirin should be used infrequently in routine primary prevention of ASCVD because of lack of net benefit [src-2019-acc-aha-guideline-on-the-primary-pr]. (Sources differ on the ASCVD-risk threshold cited for the Class IIb consideration — see Contradictions.)

**ESC (2021)** does not recommend aspirin for primary prevention in low- or moderate-risk individuals; it states aspirin may be considered only in individuals at very high cardiovascular risk, with no routine recommendation for older adults, and by implication should be discontinued unless a patient is high-risk without bleeding concerns [src-aspirin-for-primary-prevention-time-to-r][src-editorial-the-2021-european-society-of-c]. An earlier ESC stance (2016) issued a Class III B recommendation against antiplatelet therapy in those without established CVD, a position retained through 2020–2021 [src-aspirin-in-the-modern-era-of-cardiovascu].

All three contemporary guideline bodies converge on advising against routine low-dose aspirin in adults aged ≥60 for primary prevention [src-editorial-the-2021-european-society-of-c][src-potential-impact-of-the-2019-acc-aha-gui][src-daily-low-dose-aspirin-for-heart-attack].

## Subgroups and managing existing users

Current meta-analyses show no subgroup — high CV risk, diabetes, or older age — with a clear net benefit for aspirin in adults over 60 [src-aspirin-and-statin-therapy-for-primary-p]. ESC 2021 suggests aspirin may be considered for primary prevention in patients with diabetes or very high CV risk, but this rests on expert opinion rather than robust RCT evidence [src-aspirin-and-statin-therapy-for-primary-p]. For diabetes specifically, ACC/AHA may permit consideration in diabetics aged 40–70 with elevated ASCVD risk but not beyond age 70, and the evidence for net benefit in older diabetics remains uncertain [src-aspirin-for-primary-prevention-of-cardio-3][src-aspirin-in-the-prevention-of-colorectal][src-effect-of-low-dose-aspirin-on-the-elderl].

For existing users, guidelines classify routine primary-prevention aspirin in adults >70 as Class III (harm) and advise evaluating each patient's risk profile through shared decision-making [src-aspirin-and-statin-therapy-for-primary-p]. Because no robust evidence supports net benefit in any over-60 subgroup, guidelines recommend against routine initiation and encourage deprescribing where possible unless a compelling indication exists (e.g., established CVD) [src-aspirin-and-statin-therapy-for-primary-p][src-1-minute-consult-should-my-older-adult-p]. Nearly half of older adults still use aspirin for primary prevention despite guideline warnings, and clinicians should routinely review aspirin status in patients ≥60 [src-aspirin-use-for-primary-prevention-in-ol-2].

## Contradictions and open uncertainties

- **ASCEND vs. the overall picture.** ASCEND showed a statistically significant ~12% relative (1.1 percentage-point absolute) reduction in vascular events in diabetics [src-a-study-of-cardiovascular-events-in-diab][src-studies-that-changed-clinical-practice-a], yet pooled meta-analyses conclude no subgroup — including diabetes — shows a clear net benefit once bleeding is accounted for in adults over 60 [src-aspirin-and-statin-therapy-for-primary-p]. The diabetes subgroup remains the area of greatest residual uncertainty and divergent expert opinion [src-aspirin-and-statin-therapy-for-primary-p][src-effect-of-low-dose-aspirin-on-the-elderl].

- **ACC/AHA Class IIb threshold.** One source describes the Class IIb consideration as applying to adults 40–70 "at higher CVD risk" without increased bleeding risk [src-uspstf-report-on-aspirin-for-primary-pre], while another specifies a ≥20% 10-year ASCVD risk threshold [src-potential-impact-of-the-2019-acc-aha-gui], and a third (diabetes-specific) cites ≥10% [src-aspirin-for-primary-prevention-of-cardio-3]. The exact threshold is reported inconsistently across findings.

- **Age cutoff (≥70 vs. >70).** ACC/AHA's Class III (harm) designation is variously described as applying to adults "≥70" or ">70" years across sources [src-uspstf-report-on-aspirin-for-primary-pre][src-potential-impact-of-the-2019-acc-aha-gui][src-aspirin-yes-for-at-risk-elderly-but-what].

- **All-cause mortality in ASPREE.** The original ASPREE report noted a slightly higher all-cause mortality signal among aspirin users that was not confirmed in subsequent analyses; the overall conclusion is no mortality benefit [src-low-dose-aspirin-increases-risk-of-bleed][src-daily-aspirin-recommendation-should-we-a].

- **Whether to stop in current users.** The USPSTF suggests stopping around age 75 [src-uspstf-recommends-against-initiating-asp], but at least one source notes no fully explicit recommendation about continuing in those >60 and that clinicians "may continue if tolerated," even while most evidence supports discontinuation [src-aspirin-making-sense-of-changing-guideli].
